# Canonical H3 rescue–homogenisation theorem

Consider two patches with high-allele frequencies \(p_1,p_2\), and symmetric
frequency mixing at rate \(m\):

\[
p_1'=(1-m)p_1+mp_2,\qquad
p_2'=mp_1+(1-m)p_2.
\]

Subtracting the equations gives

\[
p_1'-p_2'=(1-2m)(p_1-p_2).
\]

Therefore, whenever

\[
p_1\ne p_2,\qquad 0<m<\frac12,
\]

we have

\[
|p_1'-p_2'|=(1-2m)|p_1-p_2|<|p_1-p_2|.
\]

The mean allele frequency is conserved exactly:

\[
\frac{p_1'+p_2'}2=\frac{p_1+p_2}2.
\]

Thus symmetric movement can reduce patch differentiation without changing the
metapopulation mean allele frequency.

## Coupling to demographic rescue

Frequency mixing itself is not a demographic model.  The theorem therefore
accepts a separate external-arrival rescue certificate.  When that certificate
meets its post-arrival establishment threshold, the same canonical report can
state both:

1. external arrivals rescue a recipient demographically and/or restore its
   high-trait component; and
2. movement strictly homogenises pre-existing allele-frequency differences.

This is an exact counterexample to the claim that connectivity has a uniformly
positive genetic effect.  Rescue can be beneficial for persistence while also
reducing between-patch differentiation.  Whether that reduction is desirable,
harmful, or neutral depends on the biological target and is not determined by
this theorem.
