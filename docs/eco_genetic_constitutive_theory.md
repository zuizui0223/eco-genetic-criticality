# Constitutive theory for eco-genetic criticality

## Purpose

This document is the model-building blueprint for the eco-genetic criticality
program. It is not itself a theorem, a simulation result, or a claim about any
particular ecosystem.

The distinction matters:

```text
model                = a particular formal relationship that can be analysed or simulated
constitutive theory  = propositions that specify how related models are built and compared
general theory       = broader background principles outside the present program
```

The purpose of the blueprint is to make the shared rules, domains, and scope
limits of the program's models explicit. A result from one model may only be
transferred to another after checking which propositions and additional
assumptions they share.

## Domain

The domain is systems in which patch configuration can influence interaction
support, trait performance, finite realised trait occupancy, demography, and
genetic state. The theory does **not** assert that all systems in this domain
exhibit criticality, genetic lead, or harmful fragmentation.

## Constitutive propositions

### CT1 — ecological support

Patch configuration affects local interaction support through a declared state
equation. Patch area is not assumed to act directly on every downstream state.
A model must state its path from configuration to interaction.

### CT2 — trait-space mechanism

Interaction state changes the potential performance or viability of trait
states. A model must state its trait-performance map and its high-trait region;
``high trait`` cannot be inferred merely from allele frequency.

### CT3 — potential versus realised traits

Potential trait viability and realised trait occupancy are separate state
variables. Potential viability can remain when a finite realised high-trait
bin is absent, and realised occupancy can disappear before potential viability
does.

### CT4 — demographic-genetic separation

Census abundance, effective reproductive size, allele frequency, and genetic
diversity are separate quantities. No model may substitute one for another
without an explicit mapping.

### CT5 — stochastic first passage

Finite recruitment and transmission generate random first-passage times.
Analyses must declare event thresholds, aggregation rules, censoring, and the
comparison time window before inspecting outcomes.

### CT6 — connectivity is signed, not automatically rescuing

Connectivity operates through a stated migration update. It can preserve a
common lower bound, erode a high local refuge from low sources, or rescue a low
patch from high sources. Its direction must be derived, not assumed.

### CT7 — measurement discipline

Potential viability, realised high-trait occupancy, allele persistence,
H-alpha, H-gamma, and F_ST each require separately declared observables.
A result about one is not a result about another.

## Hypotheses generated by the blueprint

### H1 — interaction criticality

Positive feedback can create a configuration-sensitive transition in interaction
state; when CT2 gives opposite high-trait viability margins across branches,
potential high-trait viability is branch-dependent.

A canonical logistic map is one model instantiation. It proves a result only
for its declared map, not for all systems satisfying CT1 and CT2.

### H2 — genetic lead

Under identifiable bounds on genetic decay and realised trait persistence,
genetic warning can precede realised high-trait loss.

The universal reading is false: CT3--CT5 permit lead, tie, trait-first, and
censored paths. L3/L4 provide conditional or probabilistic sufficient
conditions rather than an unconditional ordering.

### H3 — fixed-total-area fragmentation

At fixed total area, subdivision can change interaction support, realised trait
occupancy, and genetic structure because the model acts patchwise and because
connectivity is governed by CT6.

The universal reading is false: migration can harm or rescue depending on
source and focal bounds. The relevant comparison is between explicit landscape
models, not an area slogan.

## Current model family and relationship map

| Model instantiation | Claims it can support | Claims it cannot support |
|---|---|---|
| Canonical logistic criticality | exact branch/threshold results under its map; CT1--CT2 mechanism | finite occupancy, genetic lead, migration rescue |
| Finite-bin coupled feedback | model-specific phase diagrams and first-passage patterns | universal theorem or empirical projection |
| Conditional H-alpha lead certificate | sufficient ordering under declared bounds | automatic applicability to simulator outputs |
| No-migration stochastic refuge | finite-horizon trait-persistence probability for its submodel | arbitrary-migration network persistence |
| Migration-safe lower bound | exact effect of convex-combination migration on an allele lower bound | source-mean invariance or whole-network persistence |

This table is mirrored in
`causal_model/eco_genetic_constitutive_theory.py` and is checked by tests.

## Theory-building protocol for each new model

Before adding a theorem, simulation, or empirical projection, state:

1. Which CT propositions are instantiated?
2. What are the state variables and which are observed versus latent?
3. What is the model domain and what are its explicit exclusions?
4. Is the intended conclusion Type T (theorem), C (conditional theorem), H
   (substantive hypothesis), or S (simulation result)?
5. Which existing model does it extend, contradict, or merely coexist with?
6. What observation would distinguish the new model from the nearest existing
   alternative?

A new model that cannot answer these questions is not rejected; it is marked as
an incomplete instantiation rather than being allowed to blur the current
claims.

## Next scientific step implied by this blueprint

The next priority is not another broad simulation sweep. It is a **model
comparison theorem** for CT6:

```text
Under which source-mean and local-selection conditions does migration preserve,
erode, or rescue a high-trait refuge while changing genetic first-passage order?
```

That problem links the current no-migration refuge theorem and the exact
migration convex-combination lemma. It also creates an explicit contrast model:
connectivity-as-rescue versus connectivity-as-erosion. The future theorem must
report which extra source-region assumptions are required.

## Reference framing

This organization follows the distinction between models and constitutive
theories articulated in Scheiner (2026), especially the use of explicit
propositions to unify, compare, and generate related models. The present
blueprint does not claim to be a general theory of biology.
